Dr Andrea Loftus

Qualifications

Currently a Senior Lecturer of Psychology at Curtin University in WA, Dr Andrea Loftus is one of this lab’s founders, and its Director.

Dr Loftus coordinates courses and conducts lectures and workshops at introductory and advanced levels in Psychology and is currently teaching on a number of Health Science units within Curtin University. Dr Loftus’ aim is to provide students with the latest findings in neuroscience and management of stroke and Parkinson's- no matter how difficult the concepts may be. Dr Loftus supervises several Honours, Masters, and PhD students (including all of the students in this lab!).

Dr Loftus’ main research interests are the rehabilitation of motor and cognitive function following a stroke, and the motor and cognitive impact of Parkinson’s disease (PD). Much of this research has focussed on use-dependent re-organisation of the motor cortex and how this impacts upon movement and quality of life. Dr Loftus is further interested in the therapeutic potential of non-invasive brain stimulation techniques to improve functional outcomes for those with motor and cognitive deficits. This work has been extended to include those affected by PD, with a focus on the impact of sleep and physical exercise on motor and cognitive functioning.

Dr Loftus has an established track record with over 25 publications in peer-reviewed journals, as well as numerous national and international conference presentations in her field. Dr Loftus is also an invited reviewer for a number of behavioural neuroscience and medical journals. Dr Loftus established this research laboratory, and continues to manage and promote its work for the benefit of the community.

A/Prof Romola Bucks

Bsc. (Hons), MSc. (Clinical Psychology), PhD. (Cognitive Neuropsychology)

Currently Associate Professor at the School of Psychology, University of Western Australia, Romola obtained her qualification in Clinical Psychology at the University of Birmingham, UK, in 1991, before specializing in Clinical Neuropsychology. Romola is registered with the Psychology Board of Australia as a Registered Psychologist with an Area of Practice Endorsement in Clinical Psychology. Between 1993 and 2001, Romola was manager of the Bristol Memory Disorders Clinic and Research Centre, University of Bristol: a tertiary referral centre for the diagnosis and management of dementia, and research into diagnosis and treatment. During this time, she undertook her PhD, which was awarded in 1999.

Romola is a distinguished clinical researcher with skills in clinical research design and implementation, statistical analysis and research translation. As a CI, she has been awarded $4.1M in competitive research funding and published 85+ peer-reviewed journal articles, an edited text, 3 book chapters, and 2 neuropsychological tests. The focus of Romola’s research is on identifying risk factors for cognitive impairment and degenerative conditions, and in cognitive test development. In particular, she has expertise in sleep disorders, Type 2 diabetes, and depression. She has also written on Parkinson’s disease, Alzheimer’s disease, vascular dementia, Lewy body disease, and anxiety.

Romola is a passionate teacher, contributing at both undergraduate and postgraduate levels on topics such as research methods, ageing, dementia, and sleep disorders. She was Director of Postgraduate Training in Clinical Psychology at UWA (2012-2014). Romola has an excellent track record as a mentor and leader having supervised 46 postgraduate students (7 PhDs, 1 DPsych (HDR-Level 10), 27 DPsychs (Level 9), 8 MSc/MPhil, and 3 MPsych) and 23 Undergraduate Honours students to successful completion. Her ongoing postgraduate supervision includes 16 PhD and 1 MPhil student.

Dr Robert Kane

BA. (Hons), PhD. (Psychology)

Dr Kane’s area of expertise is in research methodology and data analysis in applied behavioural research. Dr Kane co-ordinates the primary research methods unit in Curtin University’s Masters and PhD of Clinical Psychology. The unit reviews emerging strategies in applied psychological research. The emphasis is on research design rather than statistics, and on quantitative rather than qualitative methods. Dr Kane is also the (Curtin) School of Psychology’s statistical consultant, and a statistical consultant for the journals First Language and Nursing Research. As the School's statistical consultant, he provides methodological advice to honours students, masters students, PhD students, and academic staff.

Dr Kane has provided methodological and statistical guidance as a chief investigator on one NHMRC project, two ARC projects, and four Healthway projects. These projects, which were all concerned with the evaluations of school-based interventions promoting mental health and resilience in children and adolescents, have generated 12 publications in national and international journals and the results have been disseminated at international conferences in Boston, Copenhagen, Barcelona, Florence, Mumbai, and Berlin. In 2008, on the basis of this research, Dr Kane and his colleagues were awarded Suicide Prevention Australia’s LIFE Award in the Youth Category.

In his role as a methodological and statistical advisor Dr Kane has been involved in projects investigating a diverse range of topics, including: frontotemporal dementia, psychoses, and developmental changes.

Dr Kane has previously supervised 15 PhD students to completion, and is currently involved in the supervision of another 24 PhD students.

Emily Corti

BPsych. (Hons)

Emily completed her Bachelor of Psychology (Honours) at Curtin University in 2013 as a part of the Neuroscience Research Laboratory. Emily completed her honours research project in ParkC under the supervision of Dr Andrea Loftus and Associate Professor Natalie Gasson. Emily’s research examined the relationship between personality, actual memory and perceived memory in those affected by Parkinson’s Disease. Emily has continued to work with ParkC since completing her honours project. Emily is currently employed by ParkC as the ParkC research assistant.

Emily is continuing her research career as a PhD student in the lab under the supervision of Dr Andrea Loftus and Associate Professor Natalie Gasson. Emily’s research examines neuronal activity and cognitive functioning in people with chronic lower back pain. Emily's research also examines if brain stimulation can be used to help restore normal neuronal activity and reduce the experience of pain in people with chronic lower back pain.Emily's research uses a range of non-invasive brain-stimulation techniques, including transcranial direct current stimulation (tDCS) and transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS), in her research. TMS is a pain-free, non-invasive technique that is used to measure neuronal excitability (intracortical inhibition and facilitation) using surface electromygraphic (EMG) measures of muscle activity. tDCS is a non-invasive technique that can be used to suppress or excite neuronal activity.

Emily is an undergraduate tutor for the School of Psychology at Curtin University, tutoring in Brain & Behaviour and Cognition. Emily has also been involved in the training of two honours students in the implementation of various neuropsychological assessments as a part of ParkC and her research in chronic pain. Emily has also successfully implemented a training program for the safety protocols and administration of TMS and tDCS.

Emily's Honours thesis is available here: The Impact of Personality on Actual and Self-reported Memory in Parkinson’s Disease.

Blake Lawrence

BPsych. (Hons)

Blake completed his Bachelor of Psychology (First Class Honours) at Curtin University in 2013 as part of the Neuroscience Research Laboratory. Blake’s Honours thesis examined the relationship between activities of daily living, depression, and quality of life in Parkinson’s Disease and was completed under the supervision of Dr Andrea Loftus and A/Prof Natalie Gasson. Blake published his Honours thesis as a research article in PLOS ONE, which can be found here.

Blake is now continuing his research career as a PhD candidate under the supervision of Dr Andrea Loftus and A/Prof Natalie Gasson. Blake’s PhD research investigates the individual and combined therapeutic potential of cognitive training and non-invasive brain stimulation in Parkinson’s Disease. Specifically, Blake’s research is comparing computer-based cognitive training, transcranial direct current stimulation (tDCS), and cognitive training + tDCS for improving cognition, activities of daily living, and quality of life for people with Parkinson’s Disease. tDCS is a form of non-invasive brain stimulation that delivers a low level electrical current to specific cortical regions in the brain. tDCS can be used to either excite or inhibit the brains’ neuronal activity. Computer-based cognitive training involves the repetitive use of interactive activities that require specific cognitive functions such as memory, attention, and executive function.

Blake has also been a Unit Coordinator and Lecturer for Advanced Quantitative Research Methods in the School of Psychology at Murdoch University and is currently an undergraduate tutor for Perception and Individual Differences in the School of Psychology at Curtin University. Blake also has experience training PhD students in standardised neuropsychological assessment of people with Parkinson’s Disease.

Andrew Johnson

BPsych. (Hons)

Andrew completed his Bachelor of Psychology (Honours) with the lab in 2014. He investigated the ability of physical symptoms to predict future working memory performance in Parkinson’s disease (PD), under the supervision of Dr Andrea Loftus and A/Prof Natalie Gasson.

Andrew commenced his PhD in 2015, continuing to investigate the physical presentations of Parkinson’s disease. Andrew’s PhD aims to identify the motor subtypes of PD using latent profile and latent transition analysis; a more methodologically rigorous approach than has traditionally been used. Additionally, Andrew is completing a Masters of Biostatistics through the University of Sydney; developing skills in research methods as well as data management and analysis.

Andrew was awarded the Australian Postgraduate Award and the Curtin Research Scholarship in 2015 by Curtin University for demonstrating a high level of research potential in his Honours project. Andrew continues to work to expand his research profile, with two papers under consideration and a third to be submitted soon. He is currently exploring growth modelling of tremor and postural symptoms in PD.

After completing his PhD, Andrew intends to begin a Masters of Clinical Neuropsychology, and specialise in this area.
