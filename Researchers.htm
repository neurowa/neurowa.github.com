Dr Andrea Loftus

Qualifications

Currently a Senior Lecturer of Psychology at Curtin University in WA, Dr Andrea Loftus is one of this lab’s founders, and its Director.

Dr Loftus coordinates courses and conducts lectures and workshops at introductory and advanced levels in Psychology and is currently teaching on a number of Health Science units within Curtin University. Dr Loftus’ aim is to provide students with the latest findings in neuroscience and management of stroke and Parkinson's- no matter how difficult the concepts may be. Dr Loftus supervises several Honours, Masters, and PhD students (including all of the students in this lab!).

Dr Loftus’ main research interests are the rehabilitation of motor and cognitive function following a stroke, and the motor and cognitive impact of Parkinson’s (PD). Much of this research has focussed on use-dependent re-organisation of the motor cortex and how this impacts upon movement and quality of life. Dr Loftus is further interested in the therapeutic potential of non-invasive brain stimulation techniques to improve functional outcomes for those with motor and cognitive deficits. This work has been extended to include those affected by PD, with a focus on the impact of sleep and physical exercise on motor and cognitive functioning.

Dr Loftus has an established track record with over 25 publications in peer-reviewed journals, as well as numerous national and international conference presentations in her field. Dr Loftus is also an invited reviewer for a number of behavioural neuroscience and medical journals. Dr Loftus established this research laboratory, and continues to manage and promote its work for the benefit of the community.

Dr Wei-Peng Teo

BSc., MSc., PhD.

Dr. Teo's program of research focuses on developing novel rehabilitation strategies for optimising neuromuscular health and function and improving the overall quality of life in people with neurological disorders and brain injury, particularly with PD and stroke. His doctoral thesis was one of the first to examine the brain processes involved with controlling movement speed and accuracy in healthy adults and people with PD, and further investigated the effects of non-invasive brain stimulation on motor learning in PD. Dr. Teo's PhD work was the first to confirm that motor symptoms of PD, particularly slowness in movement and the inability to produce accurate movements, have distinct neural causes that are differentially affected by medication. Further, his PhD findings indicate that an increased state of brain excitability and a reduced inhibition in the primary motor cortex may play a significant role in motor deficits in PD, and that non-invasive brain stimulation helps to improve brain excitability, resulting in an improvement in motor function.

Dr. Teo currently holds the position of an Alfred Deakin Postdoctoral Research Fellow at Deakin University and is the Lead Investigator on a ongoing clinical trial examining the use of non-invasive brain stimulation and exercise in people with PD. While there are several lines of evidence to suggest that non-invasive brain stimulation alone may improve motor symptoms in people with PD, the combined effects of exercise and brain stimulation are still unknown. The findings from this trial will allow us to better understand how non-invasive brain stimulation improves brain function in people with PD, and to hopefully identify optimal stimulation parameters to truly maximise its effects.

Blake’s PhD research investigates the individual and combined therapeutic potential of cognitive training and non-invasive brain stimulation in Parkinson’s. Specifically, Blake’s research is comparing computer-based cognitive training, transcranial direct current stimulation (tDCS), and cognitive training + tDCS for improving cognition, activities of daily living, and quality of life for people with Parkinson’s. tDCS is a form of non-invasive brain stimulation that delivers a low level electrical current to specific cortical regions in the brain. tDCS can be used to either excite or inhibit the brains’ neuronal activity. Computer-based cognitive training involves the repetitive use of interactive activities that require specific cognitive functions such as memory, attention, and executive function.

Maria Pushpanathan

BA. Pyschology (Hons)

Maria completed her Honours project at the School of Psychology at the University of Western Australia in 2009 under the supervision of Dr Andrea Loftus. Her honours thesis examined the relationship between self-reported sleep and memory in Parkinson’s.

Maria then chose to continue study in this area under the supervision of A/Prof Romola Bucks and Dr Andrea Loftus. She completed the Graduate Certificate of Adult Sleep Science at the University of Western Australia in 2011.

Maria’s PhD explores how people with Parkinson’s sleep, and how sleep may be related to cognition. The 2 main objectives of Maria’s PhD are: 1) To study how we measure sleep and cognition in Parkinson’s; to pinpoint ‘pitfalls’ of data collection; and to identify appropriate statistical techniques for analysis; and, 2) Using objective sleep measurement (overnight sleep studies), to investigate whether poor quality sleep has the same effect on cognition in Parkinson’s as in neurologically healthy age-matched controls.

Sleep disruption is one of the earliest symptoms of Parkinson’s, and contributes significantly to quality of life, mood and cognition. Thus, it is vital to ensure that data are accurate and analysed appropriately. Additionally, it is important to determine the nature of the relationships between sleep and cognition in PD; if sleep problems are causing cognitive problems, interventions to improve sleep may also improve cognition.

Thus far, Maria has 1 publication, a second in review, and 2 more in preparation. She has also presented her research at both national and international conferences, and was selected to speak at the Young Scientists’ Symposium at the Non-Motor Symptoms conference of the Movement Disorders Society, held in Nice in December 2014.

Maria's Honours thesis is available here: The Relationship between Sleep Disorders and Memory Dysfunction in People with Parkinson’s Disease
