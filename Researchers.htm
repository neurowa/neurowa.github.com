A/Prof Romola Bucks

Bsc. (Hons), MSc. (Clinical Psychology), PhD. (Cognitive Neuropsychology)

Currently Associate Professor at the School of Psychology, University of Western Australia, Romola obtained her qualification in Clinical Psychology at the University of Birmingham, UK, in 1991, before specializing in Clinical Neuropsychology. Romola is registered with the Psychology Board of Australia as a Registered Psychologist with an Area of Practice Endorsement in Clinical Psychology. Between 1993 and 2001, Romola was manager of the Bristol Memory Disorders Clinic and Research Centre, University of Bristol: a tertiary referral centre for the diagnosis and management of dementia, and research into diagnosis and treatment. During this time, she undertook her PhD, which was awarded in 1999.

Romola is a distinguished clinical researcher with skills in clinical research design and implementation, statistical analysis and research translation. As a CI, she has been awarded $4.1M in competitive research funding and published 85+ peer-reviewed journal articles, an edited text, 3 book chapters, and 2 neuropsychological tests. The focus of Romola’s research is on identifying risk factors for cognitive impairment and degenerative conditions, and in cognitive test development. In particular, she has expertise in sleep disorders, Type 2 diabetes, and depression. She has also written on Parkinson’s disease, Alzheimer’s disease, vascular dementia, Lewy body disease, and anxiety.

Romola is a passionate teacher, contributing at both undergraduate and postgraduate levels on topics such as research methods, ageing, dementia, and sleep disorders. She was Director of Postgraduate Training in Clinical Psychology at UWA (2012-2014). Romola has an excellent track record as a mentor and leader having supervised 46 postgraduate students (7 PhDs, 1 DPsych (HDR-Level 10), 27 DPsychs (Level 9), 8 MSc/MPhil, and 3 MPsych) and 23 Undergraduate Honours students to successful completion. Her ongoing postgraduate supervision includes 16 PhD and 1 MPhil student.

Emily Corti

BPsych. (Hons)

Emily completed her Bachelor of Psychology (Honours) at Curtin University in 2013 as a part of the Neuroscience Research Laboratory. Emily completed her honours research project in ParkC under the supervision of Dr Andrea Loftus and Associate Professor Natalie Gasson. Emily’s research examined the relationship between personality, actual memory and perceived memory in those affected by Parkinson’s Disease. Emily has continued to work with ParkC since completing her honours project. Emily is currently employed by ParkC as the ParkC research assistant.

Emily is continuing her research career as a PhD student in the lab under the supervision of Dr Andrea Loftus and Associate Professor Natalie Gasson. Emily’s research examines neuronal activity and cognitive functioning in people with chronic lower back pain. Emily's research also examines if brain stimulation can be used to help restore normal neuronal activity and reduce the experience of pain in people with chronic lower back pain.Emily's research uses a range of non-invasive brain-stimulation techniques, including transcranial direct current stimulation (tDCS) and transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS), in her research. TMS is a pain-free, non-invasive technique that is used to measure neuronal excitability (intracortical inhibition and facilitation) using surface electromygraphic (EMG) measures of muscle activity. tDCS is a non-invasive technique that can be used to suppress or excite neuronal activity.

Emily is an undergraduate tutor for the School of Psychology at Curtin University, tutoring in Brain & Behaviour and Cognition. Emily has also been involved in the training of two honours students in the implementation of various neuropsychological assessments as a part of ParkC and her research in chronic pain. Emily has also successfully implemented a training program for the safety protocols and administration of TMS and tDCS.

Emily's Honours thesis is available here: The Impact of Personality on Actual and Self-reported Memory in Parkinson’s Disease.

Andrew Johnson

BPsych. (Hons)

Andrew completed his Bachelor of Psychology (Honours) with the lab in 2014. He investigated the ability of physical symptoms to predict future working memory performance in Parkinson’s disease (PD), under the supervision of Dr Andrea Loftus and A/Prof Natalie Gasson.

Andrew commenced his PhD in 2015, continuing to investigate the physical presentations of Parkinson’s disease. Andrew’s PhD aims to identify the motor subtypes of PD using latent profile and latent transition analysis; a more methodologically rigorous approach than has traditionally been used. Additionally, Andrew is completing a Masters of Biostatistics through the University of Sydney; developing skills in research methods as well as data management and analysis.

Andrew was awarded the Australian Postgraduate Award and the Curtin Research Scholarship in 2015 by Curtin University for demonstrating a high level of research potential in his Honours project. Andrew continues to work to expand his research profile, with two papers under consideration and a third to be submitted soon. He is currently exploring growth modelling of tremor and postural symptoms in PD.

After completing his PhD, Andrew intends to begin a Masters of Clinical Neuropsychology, and specialise in this area.

Andrew's Honours thesis is available here: Working Memory in Parkinson’s Disease
