Chronic Lower Back Pain:

Cognitive ability, neuroplasticity, and non-invasive brain stimulation

"...for me, pain's impact on concentration, working memory, self control and social sensitivity is the most disabling aspect of the phenomenon." Anthony Cole
Chronic Pain Australia

The Project

Background:

1 in 5 Australians suffer from chronic pain, with back pain being the most common long-term health condition. In many cases of chronic lower back pain, there is little or no physical evidence of injury identified by radiographical measures. Little is understood about why pain persists in the absence of injury. Research suggests that cortical changes may contribute to the maintenance of chronic pain in the absence of injury. To address these issues, PhD Candidate Emily Corti is examining the impact of non-invasive brain stimulation on motor cortex excitability, cognition, and the pain experience in chronic lower back pain.

Methods:

Participants receive a non-invasive form of brain stimulation called Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation (TMS). TMS allows us to look at brain activity. Participants then complete a comprehensive neuropsychological evaluation to identifying any difficulties with thinking and memory abilities. Participants then complete an intervention involving a separate non-invasive brain stimulation called Transcranial Direct Current Stimulation (tDCS). Individuals are re-evaluated after completing the intervention. This will show:

  • Whether people with chronic lower back pain show changes in brain activity
  • Whether people with chronic lower back pain perform differently on memory and thinking tasks
  • Whether the non-invasive brain stimulation modulated brain activity
  • Whether the non-invasive brain stimulation improved memory and thinking performance, and
  • Whether the non-invasive brain stimulation reduced the pain experience

Progress:

After many busy months, Emily has now completed the recruitment and testing of her participants, along with data entry and data cleaning for the project. Emily will now begin data analysis and interpretation before writing the remaining chapters of her PhD thesis.
