Mild Cognitive Impairment in Parkinson's Disease

Prevalence, subtypes, and the effect of non-invasive brain stimulation and brain training

The Project

Background:

Parkinson's is traditionally viewed as a motor disorder, however individuals often present with nonmotor symptoms such as mild cognitive impairment. There is limited empirical evidence supporting pharmacological treatment of cognitive impairments in Parkinson’s and few studies have explored ways of improving cognition in Parkinson's, without medication. To address these issues, PhD Candidate Blake Lawrence is examining the therapeutic potential of nonpharmacological interventions for improving cognition, activities of daily living and quality of life in Parkinson’s.

Methods:

Participants receive a comprehensive neuropsychological evaluation to identifying common difficulties with thinking and memory abilities. Participants then complete an intervention comprised of non-invasive brain stimulation, cognitive training, or both. Individuals are evaluated after completing the intervention, and again after three months. This will show:

  • Whether the interventions improved cognitive abilities
  • Which of the interventions was the most effective
  • Whether improvements remained after an extended period without the intervention
  • Which of the interventions' improvements had the longest effect

Progress:

After many busy months, Blake has now completed the recruitment and testing of his participants, along with data entry and data cleaning for the project. Blake will now begin data analysis and interpretation before writing the remaining chapters of his PhD thesis.
